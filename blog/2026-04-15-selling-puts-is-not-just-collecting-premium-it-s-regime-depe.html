Selling Puts Is Not Just Collecting Premium — It's Regime-Dependent

Most retail traders learn about selling puts and immediately think: free money. Collect premium, stock doesn't drop, keep it all. Simple.

It's not simple. And if you treat it like it is, a single wrong-regime trade will erase months of credits in a week.

Let me show you exactly why — with today's live data as the classroom.

The Regime Is Everything

Selling puts is a short volatility, long delta strategy. That means it works with specific market conditions — and gets absolutely destroyed when those conditions reverse. You're not just betting a stock doesn't drop. You're betting that the entire environment stays cooperative.

My pipeline runs a 9-signal risk monitor and maps everything against a Global Liquidity Index (GLI) built on Michael Howell's methodology. GLI is the macro overlay that tells you whether the plumbing of global capital markets is flowing or freezing. Here's where we sit today:

Eight of nine signals green. VIX at 18.15 — elevated enough to generate real premium, not so elevated that the market is in free-fall mode where put sellers get run over. GEX positive means options dealers are long gamma, which forces them to buy dips and sell rips — a mechanical dampener on volatility. This is a put-seller's environment.

But here's what changes everything: if GLI was declining, GEX was negative, and VIX was spiking through 25 — the exact same trades would be landmines, not income.

What This Means For Your Portfolio

The question isn't "should I sell puts?" The question is "am I in the right regime to sell puts?"

Use this framework before entering any short premium trade:

1. GLI regime: Is global liquidity expanding or contracting? Contracting liquidity = elevated margin call risk = put sellers bleed.

2. GEX: Positive GEX = dealers dampen moves. Negative GEX = dealers amplify moves. You want positive.

3. VIX level and direction: 18-25 is the premium sweet spot — enough fear to pay you, not enough chaos to bury you. VIX rising through 25 is an exit signal, not an entry.

4. Risk signal count: My 9-signal monitor at 8/9 green means broad market structure is intact. If that drops to 5/9 or below, position sizing shrinks — full stop.

5. Convergence, not just premium: If you can't name why institutional money is near your strike, you don't have a trade. You have a lottery ticket.

A put-selling strategy with no regime filter is a car with no brakes. Works fine on flat roads. Devastating when the terrain changes.
